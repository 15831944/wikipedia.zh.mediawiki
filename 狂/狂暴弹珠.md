《'''狂暴弹珠'''》是一款[[街机|街机]][[电子游戏|电子游戏]]。此游戏由{{tsl|en|Mark Cerny||马克·塞尔尼}}制作并由[[雅达利游戏|雅达利游戏]]于1984年发布。玩家需使用[[轨迹球|轨迹球]]来操纵屏幕上的弹珠在规定时间内通过六个充满障碍的关卡。《狂暴弹珠》是雅达利第一款使用{{tsl|en|Atari System 1||雅达利System 1}}硬件的游戏，而且它是以[[C语言|C语言]]编写的。这也是第一款使用真[[立体声|立体声]]的游戏；之前的游戏不是用的[[单声道|单声道]]就是用的{{tsl|en|Duophonic||模拟立体声}}。

马克·塞尔尼从{{tsl|en|miniature golf||迷你高尔夫}}、[[竞速游戏|竞速游戏]]以及[[莫里茲·柯尼利斯·艾雪|莫里茲·柯尼利斯·艾雪]]的作品中汲取灵感。他使用[[极简主义|极简主义]]来处理游戏舞台及敌人外观的设计。

《狂暴弹珠》取得了商业上的成功。游戏被移植到很多平台并促进了其他游戏的发展。其续作于1991年开发并计划发行，但因内测表明游戏无法与其他作品竞争，而取消发行计划。
==概要==

《狂暴弹珠》是一款以[[等轴测投影|等轴测投影]]的[[平台游戏|平台游戏]]。 玩家以第三人称视角操纵屏幕里的弹珠。玩家以[[轨迹球|轨迹球]]控制弹珠的移动，但大多数家用版本使用[[游戏控制器|手柄]]的[[十字键|方向键]]控制。游戏的目标是让玩家在给定时间内通过六个像迷宫的等距离比赛关卡。 除第一场外，每一场剩下的时间都会顺移到下场，玩家同样可以获得一些额外时间。游戏允许两名玩家互相竞争，并给赢家奖励分及额外时间；两个玩家的时钟是独立的。<ref name="Fever"/><ref name="Retro"/><ref name="GameTrailers"/><ref name="NP-1989"/>

随着游戏进行，关卡难度逐渐提升，还引入了更多的敌人及障碍。每关都有不同的视觉主题。例如，第一关（标题为“练习”）是个比别的关卡更简单的关卡，但与其它关卡方向相反的第五关（标题为“蠢”）则有[[波尔卡点|波尔卡点]]特征。<ref name="Fever"/><ref name="Retro"/><ref name="GameTrailers"/><ref name="NP-1989"/>

==开发==
《狂暴弹珠》由[[雅达利游戏|雅达利游戏]]开发，{{tsl|en|Mark Cerny||马克·塞尔尼}}是主设计师，而鲍勃·弗拉纳甘是软件工程师。<ref name="RG-67"/>塞尔尼和弗拉纳甘都处理了游戏的编程事务。<ref name="Retro"/>游戏机使用{{tsl|en|Atari System 1||雅达利System 1}}硬件、一个可互换电路板系统、控制面板及外观设计。<ref name="PriceGuide-1"/>游戏在19英寸{{tsl|en|Electrohome||Electrohome}}牌G07型号[[阴极射线管|CRT显示器]]上显示位图图形，还使用了{{tsl|en|Motorola 68010||摩托罗拉68010}}[[中央处理器|CPU]] ，而一颗[[MOS_6502|MOS 6502]]子系统用来控制音频及投币操作。<ref name="klov"/> 《狂暴弹珠》是雅达利使用[[雅马哈|雅马哈]][[周波数变调|FM]]{{tsl|en|sound chip||音频芯片}}的首款游戏。此芯片类似于{{tsl|en|Yamaha DX7||雅马哈DX7}}[[合成器|合成器]]，可实时创建与游戏屏幕作动[[同步|同步]]的音乐。<ref name="Retro"/><ref name="ArtOfGameWorlds"/>游戏音乐由{{tsl|en|Brad Fuller (composer)||布拉德·富勒}}及哈尔·卡农花费几个月时间熟悉音频芯片功能后编写。<ref name="Retro"/>

塞尔尼和弗拉纳甘首先合作开发了基于{{tsl|en|Michael Jackson's Thriller (music video)||迈克尔·杰克逊歌曲《战栗者》}}的游戏。企划被取消后两人开始制作塞尔尼的游戏，最终做成了《狂暴弹珠》。游戏共开发了10个月。<ref name="RG-67"/>在[[1983年美国游戏业大萧条|1983年美国游戏业大萧条]]后，雅达利专注于通过使用独特的控制器及强调[[多人电子游戏|双人游戏模式]]带来独特体验的游戏开发。塞尔尼根据上述目标设计了《狂暴弹珠》。他首先受{{tsl|en|miniature golf||迷你高尔夫}}启发，并被游戏场地的地形影响球径这一特性吸引。塞尔尼开始使用雅达利数字艺术系统测试各种想法。决定使用等轴测投影网格后，塞尔尼开始设计游戏概念。他最开始想以类似迷你高尔夫的方式击球，但雅达利对此不热情。塞尔尼此后想到[[竞速游戏|竞速游戏]]，并计划同对手在长赛道上比赛。囿于技术，游戏所需物理运算无法实时处理，而塞尔尼决定将同时间赛跑作为游戏目标。<ref name="Retro"/>

摩托罗拉CPU开发套件包括一个[[C语言|C语言]][[编译器|编译器]]，两位程序员均熟悉它。雅达利进行效率评估后，同意了此语言的使用。<ref name="RG-67"/>塞尔尼和弗拉纳甘决定用C语言为《狂暴弹珠》变成的决定是把双刃剑。雅达利游戏过去以[[汇编语言|汇编语言]]编写。C语言更易于编程，但效率较低，所以游戏以30FPS的帧率运行，相照当时通行的60FPS更慢。塞尔尼决定采用轨迹球系统（由雅达利作为“Trak-Ball”销售）来给游戏提供独特的控制系统，并且针对游戏内弹珠分别上下坡时轨迹球的加减速带来的回馈感，他选择了带马达的轨迹球。原型制作时，雅达利的设计部门提醒塞尔尼，马达化轨迹球的设计有个固有的缺陷——四个支撑点中的一个与球连接不良——采用常规轨迹球更具可行性。此外，塞尔尼曾准备使用强大的客制化芯片，此芯片可让基于 [[随机存取存储器|内存]]的[[精灵图|精灵图]]由CPU激活，但可用硬件为基于[[唯讀記憶體|只读存储器]]的静态精灵图的较不先进系统。<ref name="Retro"/>

此类技术限制驱使塞尔尼简化整体设计。受[[莫里茲·柯尼利斯·艾雪|莫里茲·柯尼利斯·艾雪]]启发，他为关卡设计抽象景物。回想起来，塞尔尼将此类设计归功于其受限的艺术技巧。<ref name="Retro"/>他是类《[[终极战区|Battlezone]]》及《{{tsl|en|I, Robot (video game)||我，机器人}}》[[三维计算机图形|三维图形]]的爱好者，但他认为视觉效果缺乏定义，且他想创造一款有“坚实且干净”三维图形的游戏。<ref name="GDC2011"/>不像其它大多数街机游戏那样，关卡图像并非以[[像素|像素]]层面绘制。取而代之的是塞尔尼定义了关卡场景中每个点的高程，并将其信息存于{{tsl|en|heightmap||高度图}}数组中。关卡图形由[[光线追踪|光线追踪]]程序沿光纤路径追踪而生成。使用高度图生成关卡外观。此格式也允许塞尔尼创建阴影及使用[[反锯齿|反锯齿]]，此技术可使图形具有更平滑的外观。塞尔尼的关卡生成器允许其有更多时间以体验关卡设计。决定关卡内所需包括元素时，实用性是关键因素；诸如弹性障碍及音符跷跷板此类不工作或无法按预期出现的元素则被省略。<ref name="Retro"/>其他从游戏设计中移除的设想还有碎玻璃障碍、黑洞陷阱及弹珠移动路径中建立的路障和坑洼。<ref name="GDC2011"/>

塞尔尼的个人兴趣在企划开发过程中发生了转变，使得原始设计文档缺乏新点子。游戏敌人角色由塞尔尼及萨姆·康姆斯托克设计，而动画亦由康姆斯托克设计。考虑到技术限制，敌人尺寸必须很小。塞尔尼和康姆斯托克故意省略其面孔以给其类似关卡外观的独特设计。<ref name="Retro"/>但雅达利的管理部门建议弹珠应有个笑脸，这将使其成为像[[吃豆人|吃豆人]]的可识别角色。作为妥协，开发部门将弹珠添上了类笑脸的痕迹。<ref name="GDC2011"/>弗拉纳甘编写了个三维[[计算机模拟|物理模型]]以规定弹珠的移动并为敌人行为制作脚本[[解释器|解释器]]<ref name="RG-67"/>随着《狂暴弹珠》接近完成，雅达利的[[焦点小组|焦点测试]]组对其有积极反馈。回想起来，塞尔尼希望制作更多关卡以延长游戏寿命，但额外关卡的制作需要更多时间及硬件成本。雅达利当时遭遇了严重的财务危机，而无法使本作开发周期延长，因为此做法将导致雅达利工厂停工。<ref name="Retro"/>


==评价及影响==

《狂暴弹珠》在1984年发售后取得了商业上的成功，并被评论家肯定。<ref name="Retro"/><ref name="GDC2011"/><ref name="BetaBlues"/>大约售出了4000台机台，不久就成了最赚钱的街机游戏。但雅达利发现，发售七周后游戏便不那么赚钱了。<ref name="Retro"/><ref name="GDC2011"/> 塞尔尼认为《狂暴弹珠》仅火爆六周的原因是其{{'}}流程短。<ref name="GDC2011"/>他相信玩家对其熟练后便会转为对其它游戏感兴趣。<ref name="Retro"/>街机机台目前已很罕见。<ref name="PriceGuide-4"/>官方认定的得点世界纪录保持者是斯坦· 希热潘斯基。<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.classicarcadegaming.com/wr/guinness/index.htm|title=  Guinness records page  @  Classic Arcade Gaming (dot com)  |website=www.classicarcadegaming.com}}</ref>

许多评论家认为游戏的高难度是其吸引力的一部分。<ref name="GameTrailers"/><ref name="Dream"/>《{{tsl|en|Next Generation (magazine)||新生代}}》在1996年将《狂暴弹珠》街机版排入其“史上最好的100款游戏”之15名，并认为“MD复刻版很酷，但《狂暴弹珠》的体验根本不可能从[[手柄|手柄]]上得到。这……是一款只能在街机厅玩的游戏。”<ref>{{cite magazine|last=|first= |title=Top 100 Games of All Time |magazine={{tsl|en|Next Generation (magazine)||Next Generation}}|issue=21 |publisher={{tsl|en|Imagine Media||Imagine Media}} |date=September 1996|page=66}}</ref>2008年，[[IGN|IGN]]的李维·布坎南将本作列入少数他认为的“梦想街机”列表里，并提及游戏的难度及他游玩本作所带来的美好回忆。<ref name="Dream"/>作家约翰·塞勒斯写道，其难度是玩家被吸引的主要原因。其他吸引人的因素包括画面、视觉设计及配乐。<ref name="Fever"/> 《{{tsl|en|Retro Gamer||怀旧玩家}}》的克雷格·格兰奈尔称此游戏为有史以来最有特色的街机游戏，并赞扬其画面“纯粹而永恒”。<ref name="Retro"/>2008年，《[[吉尼斯世界纪录|吉尼斯世界纪录]]》将其列入79个技术、创意、文化上有影响力的街机游戏列表。<ref name="GWR08"/> 《狂暴弹珠》是首款使用真[[立体声|立体声]]并有可识别旋律的游戏。<ref name="GameTrailers"/><ref name="ArtOfGameWorlds"/>不列颠作曲家{{tsl|en|Paul Weir||保罗·威尔}}评论道音乐有特点，且给游戏以独特身份。<ref name="ArtOfGameWorlds"/>轨迹球若经常使用则易损是机台的通病。<ref name="Dream"/><ref name="20AtariGames"/>

《狂暴弹珠》启发了其它类本作玩法的游戏。这类游戏基于轨迹球操作并有更难的关卡；它们也经常以《狂暴弹珠》相关术语描述。<ref name="Retro"/><ref name="GWR08"/><ref name="20AtariGames"/> {{tsl|en|Krome Studios Melbourne||墨尔本浩室}}的 《{{tsl|en|Gyroscope (video game)||陀螺仪}}》及{{tsl|en|Electric Dreams Software||电子梦想软件}}的《{{tsl|en|Spindizzy (video game)||斯皮恩迪兹伊}}》是首批此类游戏； 两者均很受欢迎。<ref name="Retro"/><ref name="YS-1"/><ref name="YS-7"/>1990年，Rare发布了含有类《狂暴弹珠》元素的《{{tsl|en|Snake Rattle 'n' Roll||双蛇城}}》。.<ref name="RareHistory"/>[[超级猴子球系列|超级猴子球系列]]使用了类似的基于滚球的玩法，但添加了其它特性，如[[小游戏|小游戏]]、其它猴子角色等。<ref name="Retro"/><ref name="20AtariGames"/>《{{tsl|en|Archer Maclean's Mercury||水银}}》亦包含许多类《狂暴弹珠》元素，但有些小区别，包括改变颜色的能力、更多危机、玩家控制的是液态汞而非弹珠。

===Ports and unreleased sequel===
Beginning in 1986, the game was [[移植_(軟體)|ported]] to numerous platforms with different companies handling the conversions; several home versions were published by [[艺电|Electronic Arts]],<ref name="MG-Search"/> {{tsl|en|Tiger Electronics||Tiger Electronics}} released handheld and tabletop [[液晶显示器|LCD]] versions of the game,<ref name="PriceGuide-2"/> and it was ported to the [[红白机|Nintendo Entertainment System]] by {{tsl|en|Rare||Rare}}.<ref name="MM-NES"/> The Commodore 64 version had a secret extra level not present in other versions.<ref name="ardai198705">{{cite news | title=Titans of the Computer Gaming World / Part II of V: Ardai on Electronic Arts | work=Computer Gaming World | date=May 1987 | author=Ardai, Charles | pages=28}}</ref>

Early versions featured simplified graphics, and the different ports were met with mixed reception. John Harris of [[Gamasutra|Gamasutra]] thought the arcade's popularity fueled the sales of the home versions, while Thomas Hanley of {{tsl|en|ScrewAttack||ScrewAttack}} commented that most versions were not as enjoyable without a track ball.<ref name="GameTrailers"/> Grannell echoed similar statements about the controls and added that many had poor visuals and [[碰撞偵測|collision detection]]. He listed the [[Amiga|Amiga]], [[Game_Boy|Game Boy]], and [[Mega_Drive|Mega Drive]] ports as the better conversions, and the Sinclair [[Sinclair_ZX_Spectrum|Sinclair ZX Spectrum]], [[个人电脑|PC]], and [[Game_Boy_Advance|Game Boy Advance]] versions among the worst.<ref name="Retro"/> ''{{tsl|en|Compute!||Compute!}}'' writers called the Amiga version's graphics and gameplay "arcade-quality".<ref name="bateman198610">{{cite news | url=https://archive.org/stream/1986-10-compute-magazine/Compute_Issue_077_1986_Oct#page/n19/mode/2up | title=A Great Year For Games | work=Compute! | date=October 1986 | accessdate=9 November 2013 | author=Bateman, Selby | pages=18}}</ref> ''[[電腦遊戲世界|Computer Gaming World]]'' stated that the Amiga version was superior to the arcade original.<ref name="wagner198611">{{cite news | title=Amiga Preferences | work=Computer Gaming World | date=November 1986 | author=Wagner, Roy | pages=38}}</ref> {{tsl|en|Bruce Webster||Bruce Webster}} of ''{{tsl|en|BYTE||BYTE}}'' wrote that the graphics of the Amiga version of 《狂暴弹珠》 in December 1986 "are really amazing". While criticizing the lack of a pause function or a top scores list, he stated that it "is definitely worth having if you own an Amiga".<ref name="webster198612">{{cite news | url=https://archive.org/stream/byte-magazine-1986-12/1986_12_BYTE_11-13_Graphics_Algorithms#page/n319/mode/2up | title=Season's Greetings | work=BYTE | date=December 1986 | accessdate=9 May 2015 | author=Webster, Bruce | pages=305}}</ref> {{tsl|en|Bil Herd||Bil Herd}} recalled that the Amiga version was so popular at [[康懋达国际|Commodore International]] that employees stole the required memory expansion from colleagues' computers to run the game. He wrote "Not an Amiga" on his {{tsl|en|Commodore 128D||Commodore 128D}} so others would not open it looking for Amiga memory.<ref name="herd20170429">{{Cite web |url=https://np.reddit.com/r/c128/comments/682da4/c128_ama_from_bil_herd/dgx5875/?context=3 |title=C128 AMA from Bil Herd |last=Herd |first=Bil |authorlink=Bil Herd |website=/r/c128 |publisher=Reddit |publication-date=2017-04-29}}</ref> ''{{tsl|en|Info (magazine)||Info}}'' gave the Amiga version four-plus stars out of five, describing it as "a totally faithful adaptation".<ref name="info198701amiga">{{Cite magazine |last=Dunnington |first=Benn |last2=Brown |first2=Mark R. |last3=Malcolm |first3=Tom |date=January-February 1987 |title=Amiga Gallery |url=https://archive.org/stream/info-magazine-13/Info_Issue_13_1987_Jan-Feb#page/n89/mode/2up |magazine=Info |pages=90-95}}</ref> It gave the Commodore 64 version three-plus stars out of five, describing it as "just a shadow of the arcade original and the excellent Amiga version" and inferior to ''{{tsl|en|Spindizzy (video game)||Spindizzy}}''. The magazine liked the graphics but criticized "marbles that handle like intoxicated turtles".<ref name="info198701">{{Cite magazine |last=Dunnington |first=Benn |last2=Brown |first2=Mark R. |last3=Malcolm |first3=Tom |date=January-February 1987 |title=64/128 Gallery |url=https://archive.org/stream/info-magazine-13/Info_Issue_13_1987_Jan-Feb#page/n13/mode/2up |magazine=Info |pages=14-21}}</ref> ''[[龙_(杂志)|Dragon]]''{{'}}s three reviewers—Hartley, Patricia, and Kirk Lesser—praised the {{tsl|en|Apple IIGS||Apple II{{sc|GS}}}} port, calling it a "must have" title for arcade fans.<ref name="Dragon131"/>

''{{tsl|en|MegaTech||MegaTech}}'' reviewers rated the Sega Mega Drive release a favorable 73%.<ref name="MegaTech"/> In 2003, 《狂暴弹珠》 was included in the multi-platform title ''{{tsl|en|Midway Arcade Treasures||Midway Arcade Treasures}}'', a compilation of classic games developed by [[WMS产业|Williams Electronics]], {{tsl|en|Midway||Midway Games}}, and Atari Games.<ref name="IGN-MAT"/> Electronic Arts released a [[移动电话|mobile phone]] port in 2010 that includes additional levels with different themes and new items that augment the gameplay.<ref name="EA-MM"/><ref name="CNET-Mobile"/> 《狂暴弹珠》 was also included in the 2012 multi-platform compilation ''{{tsl|en|Midway Arcade Origins||Midway Arcade Origins}}''.<ref>{{cite web | url = http://www.ign.com/articles/2012/11/14/midway-arcade-origins-review| first = Samuel| last = Claiborn| title = Squeezing 30 fridge-sized games onto a disc proves difficult| website = IGN| publisher = IGN Entertainment, Inc.| date = November 13, 2012| accessdate = July 20, 2014}}</ref> A port of the game is also playable in {{tsl|en|Lego Dimensions||Lego Dimensions}} via an Arcade Dock in the level "Prime Time" and in the Midway Arcade World after unlocking it.

The ''Info'' review concluded, "Our only hope is that someone, somewhere, may be working (at this very moment) on ... MARBLE MADNESS II !!!"{{r|info198701amiga}} An arcade sequel titled ''Marble Man: Marble Madness II'' was planned for release in 1991, though 塞尔尼 was uninvolved in the development.<ref name="Retro"/><ref name="BetaBlues"/> Development was led by Bob Flanagan who designed the game based on what he felt made 《狂暴弹珠》 a success in the home console market. Because the market's demographic was a younger audience, Flanagan wanted to make the sequel more accessible and introduced "Marble Man", which expanded upon the gameplay of the original game. The design also included pinball mini-games between sets of levels and allowed up to three players to traverse isometric courses. Its final wave is called "King of the Mountain". Flanagan intended to address the short length of the first game and, with the help of Mike Hally, developed seventeen courses.<ref name="Retro-55"/>

Atari created prototypes for location testing, but the game did not fare well against more popular titles at the time such as ''[[街頭霸王II_－世界勇士－|Street Fighter II]]''. Atari assumed the track balls accounted for the poor reception and commissioned a second model with joystick controls. Because the new models were met with the same poor reception, production was halted and the focus shifted to ''{{tsl|en|Guardians of the 'Hood||Guardians of the 'Hood}}'', a [[清版动作游戏|beat 'em up]] game.<ref name="BetaBlues"/><ref name="MM2"/> An [[都市傳說|urban legend]] states that when [[WMS产业|WMS Industries]] purchased {{tsl|en|Time Warner Interactive||Time Warner Interactive}} (the company which Atari Games had since merged into) in 1996, they ran a {{tsl|en|steamroller||steamroller}} over the ''Marble Madness II'' boards in order to clear inventory for tax purposes. In actuality, no ''Marble Madness II'' boards were made apart from the prototypes, and according to 塞尔尼 at most 12 of these exist.<ref>{{cite magazine|last= |first= |title=Williams 'Cements' Reputation as Ball Breaker |magazine={{tsl|en|Next Generation (magazine)||Next Generation}}|issue=26|publisher={{tsl|en|Imagine Media||Imagine Media}} |date=February 1997|page=43}}</ref> These prototypes have since become {{tsl|en|Collectable||collector items}}.<ref name="PriceGuide-3"/>
