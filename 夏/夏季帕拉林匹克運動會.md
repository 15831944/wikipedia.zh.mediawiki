'''夏季帕拉林匹克運動會'''或者'''帕運會'''，是一种由存在身体障碍的運動員參與的[[国际|国际]][[綜合運動會|綜合運動會]]。帕運會各赛事分為不同障礙等級，包含[[截肢|截肢]]、[[視力障礙|視力障礙]]、[[腦麻痺|腦麻痺]]等。[[残奥会|残奥会]]每4年舉行一次，由[[國際帕拉林匹克委員會|國際帕拉林匹克委員會]]主辦。獎勵授予則是追隨自1904年[[奥林匹克运动会|奥林匹克运动会]]，第一為[[金牌|金牌]]，第二為[[银牌|银牌]]，第三為[[铜牌|铜牌]]。

[[美国|美国]]和[[英国|英国]]都主辦2次帕運會。[[澳大利亚|澳大利亚]]、[[加拿大|加拿大]]、[[中华人民共和国|中國]]、[[希腊|希腊]]、[[以色列|以色列]]、[[意大利|意大利]]、[[日本|日本]]、[[荷兰|荷兰]]、[[大韩民国|南韓]]、[[西班牙|西班牙]]和[[西德|西德]]也主办过残奥会。在[[2016年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會|2016年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會]]，由[[巴西|巴西]][[里约热内卢|里约热内卢]]主持，是第一個南美洲主辦夏季賽事。[[東京|東京]]將是第一個同一城市舉辦兩次的城市：[[1964年夏季残奥会|1964]]及[[2020年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會|2020]]。

[[帕拉林匹克運動會阿根廷代表團|阿根廷]]、[[帕拉林匹克運動會澳洲代表團|澳洲]]、[[帕拉林匹克運動會奧地利代表團|奧地利]]、[[帕拉林匹克運動會比利時代表團|比利時]]、[[帕拉林匹克運動會法國代表團|法國]]、[[帕拉林匹克運動會英國代表團|英國]]、[[帕拉林匹克運動會愛爾蘭代表團|愛爾蘭]]、[[帕拉林匹克運動會以色列代表團|以色列]]、[[帕拉林匹克運動會義大利代表團|義大利]]、[[帕拉林匹克運動會荷蘭代表團|荷蘭]]、[[帕拉林匹克運動會瑞士代表團|瑞士]]、[[帕拉林匹克運動會美國代表團|美國]] 等12个国家參與了每屆夏季帕運。[[帕拉林匹克運動會澳洲代表團|澳洲]]、[[帕拉林匹克運動會奧地利代表團|奧地利]]、[[帕拉林匹克運動會法國代表團|法國]]、[[帕拉林匹克運動會英國代表團|英國]]、[[帕拉林匹克運動會義大利代表團|義大利]]、[[帕拉林匹克運動會荷蘭代表團|荷蘭]]及[[帕拉林匹克運動會美國代表團|美國]]七個國家每屆都获得金牌：

美國曾有過8次帕運會獎牌榜第一的紀錄：[[1964年夏季残奥会|1964]]、[[1968年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會|1968]]、[[1976年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會|1976]]、[[1980年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會|1980]]、[[1984年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會|1984]]、[[1988年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會|1988]]、[[1992年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會|1992]]、[[1996年夏季残奥会|1996]]。[[残奥会中华人民共和国代表团|中國]]則有4次帕運會獎牌榜第一紀錄： [[2004年夏季残奥会|2004]]、[[2008年夏季残奥会|2008]]、[[2012年夏季残奥会|2012]]和[[2016年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會|2016]]。義大利([[1960年夏季残奥会|1960]])、{{tsl|en|West Germany at the Paralympics||西德}} ([[1972年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會|1972]])、澳大利亚([[2000年夏季残奥会|2000]])則各一次。

==資格==
殘疾人體育運動的資格規則由{{tsl|en|sport governing body||國際聯合會}} (IF)制定，该组织負責該運動的國際比賽。

==歷史==

第一屆公認的帕運會為[[1960年夏季残奥会|1960年]]的[[罗马|罗马]]帕運會。<ref>[http://www.cbc.ca/olympics/story/2008/09/02/f-paralympics-history.html "Paralympics traces roots to Second World War"], CBC, September 3, 2008</ref>約400運動員及23國家參與該次帕運，當時只有輪椅使用者參與。

[[1976年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會|1976年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會]]有不同的殘疾的運動員被第一次包括在夏季殘奧會。同時隨著更多殘疾分級的列入，1976年夏季運動會擴大到來自40個國家的1600名運動員。<ref name=winterhistory>{{cite web|title=History of the Paralympic Games |publisher=Government of Canada |url=http://www.canada2010.gc.ca/101/histor/010203-eng.cfm |accessdate=2010-04-07 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20100312224915/http://www.canada2010.gc.ca/101/histor/010203-eng.cfm |archivedate=2010-03-12 }}</ref>

[[1988年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會|1988年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會]]是第一個在[[1988年夏季奥林匹克运动会|當年奧運會]]同一地點（因此使用相同的設施）。從此帕運會就在與奧運會同一個地點舉行，與奧運會有2個星期的差距。

[[2016年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會|2016年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會]]由[[巴西|巴西]][[里约热内卢|里约热内卢]]舉辦，成為第一個在[[拉丁美洲|拉丁美洲]]及[[南美洲|南美洲]]舉行的帕運賽事。[[日本|日本]][[東京|東京]]將舉辦[[2020年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會|2020年夏季帕拉林匹克運動會]]成為第一個舉辦兩次帕運會賽事的國家。

==分類==
[[File:Wheelchair_basketball_at_the_2008_Summer_Paralympics.jpg|thumb]]

參賽者的伤残状况分為十種：肌肉力量受損、動作範圍限制，肢體不全，腿長差異、{{tsl|en|short stature||short stature}}、[[張力亢進|張力亢進]]、[[失調|失調]]、{{tsl|en|athetosis||athetosis}}、[[視力障礙|視力障礙]]和{{tsl|en|intellectual impairment||intellectual impairment}}。<ref>http://www.paralympic.org/classification</ref>依据具體的實際競爭要求，每個[[残奥会|帕運會項目]]有自己的分類。項目的編號由數字和字母組成，描述競賽類型和運動員的分類。有些項目如[[2008年夏季残奥会田径比赛|2008年夏季残奥会田径比赛]]，除了殘疾人的類別和嚴重程度以外，其他項目如[[2008年夏季残奥会游泳比赛|2008年夏季残奥会游泳比赛]]，來自不同類別的集體競爭對手一起，唯一的分別是基於殘疾的嚴重性。<ref name=class3>{{cite news |title=A-Z of Paralympic classification |publisher=[[英国广播公司|英国广播公司]] |date=28 August 2008 |url=http://news.bbc.co.uk/sport1/hi/other_sports/disability_sport/7586684.stm |accessdate=25 December 2015}}</ref>在十個殘疾類別中，運動員仍然需要根據其損傷程度進行分割。 分類系統不同於體育運動，而是打算甚至運動場地，以允許盡可能多的運動員參與。 分類根據執行運動所需的不同技能而有所不同。

'''{{tsl|en|Archery at the Summer Paralympics||射箭}}''':
Archery is open to athletes with a physical disability. Classifications are broken up into three divisions: W1, [[脊髓|spinal cord]] injured and [[腦麻痺|Cerebral Palsy]] athletes with impairment in all four limbs. W2, wheelchair users with full arm function. W3, standing amputee, ''Les Autres'' and Cerebral Palsy standing athletes. Some athletes in the standing group will sit on a high stool for support but will still have their feet touching the ground.<ref>{{cite web|title=Archery |publisher=Australian Paralympic Committee |url=http://www.paralympic.org.au/Sport/SportProfiles/Archey.aspx |accessdate=2010-04-07 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/20090913021530/http://www.paralympic.org.au/Sport/SportProfiles/Archey.aspx |archivedate=September 13, 2009 }}</ref>

'''{{tsl|en|Athletics at the Summer Paralympics||田徑}}''':
Athletics is open to all disability groups and uses a functional classification system. A brief classification guide is as follows: prefixing F for field athletes or T for track athletes. F or T 11–13 are visually impaired, F or T 20 are learning disabled, F or T 32–38 are Cerebral Palsy, F or T 40–46 amputee and ''Les Autres'', T 51–54 wheelchair track athletes and F 51–58 wheelchair field athletes.<ref>{{cite web|title=Athletics |publisher=Australian Paralympic Committee |url=http://www.paralympic.org.au/Sport/SportProfiles/Athletics.aspx |accessdate=2010-04-07 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20100118115126/http://www.paralympic.org.au/Sport/SportProfiles/Athletics.aspx |archivedate=January 18, 2010 }}</ref>

'''{{tsl|en|Wheelchair basketball at the Summer Paralympics||籃球}}''':
Basketball is open to wheelchair athletes. Wheelchair athletes are classified according to their physical ability and are given a points rating between 0.5 – 4.5. The individuals who rate at 0.5 are the most severely disabled and those at 4.5 are the least disabled. A team on the court comprises five players and may not exceed a total of 14 points at any given time.<ref>{{cite web|title=Basketball|publisher=International Paralympic Committee|url=http://www.paralympic.org/Sport/IF_Sports/Wheelchair_Basketball/index.html|accessdate=2010-04-07}}</ref>

'''{{tsl|en|Boccia at the Summer Paralympics||地板滾球}}''':
Boccia is open to athletes with cerebral palsy or related neurological conditions who compete from a wheelchair. Classifications are split into four groups; BC1: Athletes are either throwers or foot players (with Cerebral Palsy). Athletes may compete with an assistant 
BC2: For throwing players (with Cerebral Palsy). Players may not have an assistant
BC3: Athletes (with severe disability) who use an assistive device and may be assisted by a person, but this assistant must keep their back to the court.
BC4: For throwing players. Players may not have an assistant (non Cerebral Palsy).<ref>{{cite web|title=Boccia rules of play|publisher=Cerebral Palsy International Sports and Recreation Association|format=PDF|pages=6–8|url=http://www.cpisra.org/files/manual10p/CPISRA_Sports_Manual_10th_Edition_Section_B_Sports_Rules_Boccia_2010-02_Release_006.pdf|accessdate=2010-04-07}}</ref>

'''{{tsl|en|Cycling at the Summer Paralympics||自由車}}''':
Cycling is open to amputee, ''Les Autre'', Cerebral Palsy and visually impaired athletes who compete in individual road race and track events. Classifications are broken up into divisions 2, 3 and 4.  Athletes in division two are the most severely disabled. While athletes in division four are considered to be higher functioning.  Visually impaired athletes compete together with no separate classification system. They ride in tandem with a sighted guide. Amputee, spinal cord injury and ''Les Autre'' competitors compete within the classification groupings LC1 – for riders with upper limb disabilities, LC2 – for riders with disabilities in one leg but who are able to pedal normally, LC3 – essentially for riders with a handicap in one lower limb who will usually pedal with one leg only, and LC4 for riders with disabilities affecting both legs.<ref>{{cite web|title=Cycling |publisher=Australian Paralympic Committee |url=http://www.paralympic.org.au/Sport/SportProfiles/Cycling.aspx |accessdate=2010-04-07 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/20100304174015/http://www.paralympic.org.au:80/Sport/SportProfiles/Cycling.aspx |archivedate=March 4, 2010 }}</ref>

'''{{tsl|en|Equestrian at the Summer Paralympics||馬術}}''':
Equestrian is open to all disability groups, with riders divided into four grades. Grade 1 incorporates severely disabled riders with Cerebral Palsy, ''Les Autres'' and spinal cord injury. Grade 2 incorporates Cerebral Palsy, ''Les Autres'', spinal cord injury and amputee riders with reasonable balance and abdominal control. Grade 3 is for Cerebral Palsy, ''Les Autres'', amputee, spinal cord injury and totally blind athletes with good balance, leg movement and co-ordination. Grade 4 incorporates athletes who have Cerebral Palsy, ''Les Autres'', amputation(s), spinal cord injury and/or are visually impaired. This last group comprises ambulant athletes with either impaired vision or impaired arm/leg function.<ref>{{cite web|title=Equestrian |publisher=Australian Paralympic Committee |url=http://www.paralympic.org.au/Sport/SportProfiles/Equestrian.aspx |accessdate=2010-04-07 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/20100304174011/http://www.paralympic.org.au:80/Sport/SportProfiles/Equestrian.aspx |archivedate=March 4, 2010 }}</ref>

'''{{tsl|en|Wheelchair fencing at the Summer Paralympics||擊劍}}''':
Fencing is open to wheelchair athletes. There are only three classes; class A incorporates those athletes with good balance and recovery and full trunk movement; class B is for those with poor balance and recovery but full use of one or both upper limbs; class C is for athletes with severe physical impairment in all four limbs.<ref>{{cite web|title=Fencing Classification Rules|publisher=International Wheelchair and Amputee Sports Federation|url=http://www.iwasf.com/iwasf/assets/File/Fencing/IWF_Classification_Rules.pdf|format=PDF|page=10|accessdate=2010-04-07}}</ref>

'''[[夏季残奥会五人制足球比赛|五人制足球]]''':
There are two forms of football played at the Paralympics.  The first is 5-a-side football, which is open to visually impaired athletes.  The second is 7-a-side football, which is open to athletes with Cerebral Palsy.  5-a-side football is open to all visually impaired athletes.  Since there are different levels of visual impairment, all players except the goalie (who acts as a guide) are required to wear eye shades.  The field dimensions are smaller than able-bodied football, there are only five players on the pitch and the ball makes a sound.  Otherwise the rules are exactly the same as able-bodied football.<ref>{{cite web|title=Football 5-a-side|publisher=International Paralympic Committee|url=http://www.paralympic.org/Sport/IOSD_Sports/Football_5-a-side/index.html|accessdate=2010-04-08}}</ref>Athletes competing in 7-a-side football are broken down into classes 5, 6, 7 and 8. All classes comprise ambulant athletes; class 5 being the least physically able, progressing through to class 8 who are minimally affected. Teams must include at least one athlete from either class 5 or 6.  Furthermore, no more than three players from class 8 are allowed to play at the same time.  Other than the fact that the game is played with seven players the rest of the rules and dimensions of the playing field are the same as able-bodied football.<ref>{{cite web|title=Football |publisher=Australian Paralympic Committee |url=http://www.paralympic.org.au/Sport/SportProfiles/Football.aspx |accessdate=2010-04-08 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/20100323172403/http://www.paralympic.org.au:80/Sport/SportProfiles/Football.aspx |archivedate=March 23, 2010 }}</ref>

'''{{tsl|en|Goalball at the Summer Paralympics||門球}}''':
Goalball is open to visually impaired athletes who must wear "black out" masks to ensure all participants can compete equally, therefore eliminating the need for classification.  The ball has a bell in it to help the players react to the ball.  Complete silence at the venue is required so that the athletes can orient themselves and to ensure fairness.<ref>{{cite web|title=Goalball|publisher=International Paralympic Committee|url=http://www.paralympic.org/Sport/IOSD_Sports/Goalball/index.html|accessdate=2010-04-08}}</ref>

'''[[盲人柔道|柔道]]''':
Judo is open to visually impaired athletes. The rules are the same as able-bodied judo except that the players are allowed contact with their opponent prior to the start of the match.  There are no classifications; participants are divided into weight categories in the same way as able-bodied judo athletes.<ref>{{cite web|title=Judo|publisher=International Paralympic Committee|url=http://www.paralympic.org/Sport/IOSD_Sports/Judo/index.html|accessdate=2010-04-08}}</ref>

'''{{tsl|en|Powerlifting at the Summer Paralympics||舉重}}''':
Powerlifting is open to athletes with Cerebral Palsy, spinal cord injuries, amputations (lower limb only), and ''Les Autres''.  Since the competition is a test of upper body strength the classifications are by weight category as in able-bodied powerlifting competition.<ref>{{cite web|title=Powerlifting |publisher=Australian Paralympic Committee |url=http://www.paralympic.org.au/Sport/SportProfiles/Powerlifting.aspx |accessdate=2010-04-08 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/20090912230239/http://www.paralympic.org.au/Sport/SportProfiles/Powerlifting.aspx |archivedate=September 12, 2009 }}</ref>

'''{{tsl|en|Sailing at the Summer Paralympics||帆船}}''':
Sailing is open to amputee, Cerebral Palsy, visually impaired, spinal cord injured and ''Les Autres'' athletes. There are three events, one for single, double, and triple-crew boats.  Classification for sailing in the three-person event is based on a functional points system with low points for severely disabled athletes rising by scale to high points for less disabled athletes. A classification committee evaluates each sailor and assign a point from one to seven based on their level of ability.  Each crew of three is allowed a maximum of 14 points. The single-person event can be crewed regardless of points but the sailor must have at least a minimum level of disability which prevents them from competing on equal terms with able-bodied sailors.  The two-person event is designed for more severely disabled athletes.<ref>{{cite web|title=Sailing |publisher=Australian Paralympic Committee |url=http://www.paralympic.org.au/Sport/SportProfiles/Sailing.aspx |accessdate=2010-04-08 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/20100323172408/http://www.paralympic.org.au:80/Sport/SportProfiles/Sailing.aspx |archivedate=March 23, 2010 }}</ref>

'''{{tsl|en|Shooting at the Summer Paralympics||射擊}}''':
Shooting is open to athletes with a physical disability. There are only two classes of competition, wheelchair and standing.  There are two types of events, pistol and rifle.  The athletes are broken down into classes based on their upper body functionality, balance, muscle strength and limb mobility.  The three classes are SH1-competitors do not require a shooting stand, SH2-competitors cannot support the weight of the gun and require a shooting stand, and SH3-Rifle competitors with a visual impairment.<ref>{{cite web|title=Shooting |publisher=Australian Paralympic Committee |url=http://www.paralympic.org.au/Sport/SportProfiles/Shooting.aspx |accessdate=2010-04-08 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/20100323171902/http://www.paralympic.org.au:80/Sport/SportProfiles/Shooting.aspx |archivedate=March 23, 2010 }}</ref>

[[File:Swimming_at_the_2008_Summer_Paralympics_-_women_Butterfly_stroke.jpg|thumb]]
'''{{tsl|en|Swimming at the Summer Paralympics||游泳}}''':
The Paralympic swimming competition features all four of the strokes used in able-bodied swimming competitions.  Classification is divided into three groups: S1 to S10 are those with physical impairment. S1 will have the most severe impairment and an S10 a lesser impairment. Athletes are judged on their muscle strength, joint range of motion, limb length and movement co-ordination.  S11 to S13 are those with a visual impairment. S11 will have little or no vision, S12 can recognise the shape of a hand and have some ability to see, S13 greater vision than the other two classes but less than 20 degrees of vision. S14 is for athletes with a learning difficulty.<ref>{{cite web|title=Swimming |publisher=Australian Paralympic Committee |url=http://www.paralympic.org.au/Sport/SportProfiles/Swimming.aspx |accessdate=2010-04-08 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/20100323172424/http://www.paralympic.org.au:80/Sport/SportProfiles/Swimming.aspx |archivedate=March 23, 2010 }}</ref>

'''{{tsl|en|Table tennis at the Summer Paralympics||桌球}}''':
Table tennis is open to athletes with a physical disability. There are individual, doubles and team events.  A match is 5 sets of 11 points each.  The athletes are broken down into ten divisions based on their level of function.  Classes 1 to 5 are for athletes competing from a wheelchair with class 1 being the most severely disabled and class 5 the least disabled. Classes 6 to 10 encompass ambulant athletes with class 6 the most severely disabled and class 10 the least.<ref>{{cite web|title=Table Tennis |publisher=Australian Paralympic Committee |url=http://www.paralympic.org.au/Sport/SportProfiles/TableTennis.aspx |accessdate=2010-04-08 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/20100323171907/http://www.paralympic.org.au:80/Sport/SportProfiles/TableTennis.aspx |archivedate=March 23, 2010 }}</ref>

'''{{tsl|en|Wheelchair tennis at the Summer Paralympics||網球}}''':
Tennis at the Paralympics is played with all the same rules as able-bodied tennis with the exception that the ball is allowed to bounce twice, and the first bounce must be within the bounds of the court.  It is open to athletes with a mobility related disability which means that they cannot compete on equal terms with able-bodied tennis players. The game is played from a wheelchair, with two classes, paraplegic (at least one leg must have a permanent and substantial loss of function) and quadriplegic (at least three limbs must have a permanent and substantial loss of function).<ref>{{cite web|title=Wheelchair Tennis|publisher=International Paralympic Committee|url=http://www.paralympic.org/Sport/IF_Sports/Wheelchair_Tennis/|accessdate=2010-04-08}}</ref>

'''{{tsl|en|Volleyball at the Summer Paralympics||排球}}''':
Volleyball is open to athletes with a physical disability and is performed from a seated position. In sitting volleyball the court is smaller than the standard court and has a lower net. In the sitting games the only classification rule is that each team may have only one player who fits the minimum disability rule, which is that their disability prevents them from competing on equal terms with able-bodied athletes.  The other players on the team must demonstrate a higher level of disability.<ref>{{cite web|title=Volleyball |publisher=Australian Paralympic Committee |url=http://www.paralympic.org.au/Sport/SportProfiles/Volleyball.aspx |accessdate=2010-04-08 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/20100323172430/http://www.paralympic.org.au:80/Sport/SportProfiles/Volleyball.aspx |archivedate=March 23, 2010 }}</ref>

'''{{tsl|en|Wheelchair rugby at the Summer Paralympics||輪椅橄欖球}}''':
Athletes are classified on a points system similar to wheelchair basketball, with the most severely disabled athlete being graded at 0.5 points rising to 3.5 points. Each team has four players and is allowed a maximum of eight points on the court at any one time.<ref>{{cite web|title=Wheelchair Rugby|publisher=International Paralympic Committee|url=http://www.paralympic.org/Sport/IF_Sports/Wheelchair_Rugby/index.html|accessdate=2010-04-08}}</ref>
