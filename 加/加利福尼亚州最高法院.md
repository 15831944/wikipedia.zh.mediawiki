'''加利福尼亚州最高法院'''（Supreme Court of California）是[[加利福尼亚州|加利福尼亚州]]地区司法系统的[[终审法院|终审法院]]。法庭于[[三藩市|三藩市]]沃伦法院大楼办公，并会于[[洛城|洛城]]及[[沙加缅度|沙加缅度]]的办公大楼进行审讯。加州最高法院的判决于加州所有法院具具约束力。
<ref>''Auto Equity Sales, Inc. v. Superior Court'', [http://online.ceb.com/calcases/C2/57C2d450.htm 57 Cal. 2d 450] (1962).  In ''Auto Equity Sales'', the Court explained: "Under the doctrine of ''[[stare_decisis|stare decisis]]'', all tribunals exercising inferior jurisdiction are required to follow decisions of courts exercising superior jurisdiction. Otherwise, the doctrine of ''stare decisis'' makes no sense. The decisions of this court are binding upon and must be followed by all the state courts of California. Decisions of every division of the District Courts of Appeal are binding upon all the justice and municipal courts and upon all the superior courts of this state, and this is so whether or not the superior court is acting as a trial or appellate court. Courts exercising inferior jurisdiction must accept the law declared by courts of superior jurisdiction. It is not their function to attempt to overrule decisions of a higher court."</ref>

==組成人員==
根據最初1849年的[[加利福尼亞州憲法|加利福尼亞州憲法]]，最高法院開始時是由一名首席[[大法官|大法官]]和兩名大法官所組成。<ref>{{cite web|title=Records of the Constitutional Convention of 1849|url=http://archives.cdn.sos.ca.gov/collections/1849/images/const10.jpg|publisher=California Secretary of State|accessdate=September 27, 2017}} Art. VI, Sec. 3.</ref><!--The Court was expanded to five justices in 1862.  Under the current 1879 constitution, the Court expanded to six associate justices and one chief justice, for the current total of seven.<ref>{{cite web|title=California Constitution|url=https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displayText.xhtml?lawCode=CONS&division=&title=&part=&chapter=&article=VI|publisher=California Legislature|accessdate=September 27, 2017}}
 Art. VI, Sec. 2, 16(a).</ref> The justices are appointed by the [[Governor_of_California|Governor of California]] and are subject to retention elections.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Levinson|first1=Jessica A.|title=Op-Ed: Why voters shouldn't be electing judges|url=http://www.latimes.com/opinion/op-ed/la-oe-levinson-end-judicial-elections-20140509-story.html|accessdate=June 6, 2017|work=Los Angeles Times|date=May 8, 2014}}</ref>

According to the California Constitution, to be considered for appointment, a person must be an attorney admitted to practice in California or have served as a judge of a California court for 10 years immediately preceding the appointment.<ref>{{Cite web|title = Codes Display Text|url = http://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displayText.xhtml?lawCode=CONS&division=&title=&part=&chapter=&article=VI|website = leginfo.legislature.ca.gov|access-date = 2016-02-07}}</ref>

To fill a vacant position, the Governor must first submit a candidate's name to the Commission on Judicial Nominees Evaluation of the State Bar of California, which prepares and returns a thorough, confidential evaluation of the candidate.<ref>{{cite web|title=Commission on Judicial Nominees Evaluation|url=http://www.calbar.ca.gov/About-Us/Who-We-Are/Committees-Commissions/Judicial-Nominees-Evaluation|publisher=State Bar of California|accessdate=June 6, 2017}}</ref> Next, the Governor officially nominates the candidate, who must then be evaluated by the Commission on Judicial Appointments, which consists of the [[Chief_Justice_of_California|Chief Justice of California]], the [[Attorney_General_of_California|Attorney General of California]], and a senior presiding justice of the [[California_Courts_of_Appeal|California Courts of Appeal]].<ref>{{cite web|title=Commission on Judicial Appointments|url=http://www.courts.ca.gov/5367.htm|publisher=California Courts|accessdate=June 6, 2017}}</ref> The Commission holds a public hearing and if satisfied with the nominee's qualifications, confirms the nomination. The nominee can then immediately fill an existing vacancy, or replace a departing justice at the beginning of the next judicial term.

If a nominee is confirmed to fill a vacancy that arose partway through a judicial term, the justice must stand for retention during the next gubernatorial election. Voters then determine whether to retain the justice for the remainder of the judicial term. At the term's conclusion, justices must again undergo a statewide retention election for a full 12-year term.<ref>{{cite constitution |article=VI |section=16 |country=California |language= |ratified=1879 |url=http://www.leginfo.ca.gov/.const/.article_6 |accessdate=2014-12-25 }}</ref> If a majority votes "no," the seat becomes vacant and may be filled by the Governor.

The electorate has occasionally exercised the power not to retain justices.<ref>{{cite web|title=Judicial Selection in the States|url=http://www.judicialselection.us/judicial_selection/index.cfm?state=|website=Judicialselection.us|publisher=National Center for State Courts|accessdate=June 6, 2017}}</ref> Chief Justice [[Rose_Bird|Rose Bird]] and Associate Justices [[Cruz_Reynoso|Cruz Reynoso]] and [[Joseph_Grodin|Joseph Grodin]] were staunchly opposed to [[Capital_punishment_in_the_United_States|capital punishment]] and were subsequently removed in the 1986 general election.<ref>{{cite web|title=What Does California’s Experience with Recall of Judges Teach Us?|url=http://scocablog.com/what-does-californias-experience-with-recall-of-judges-teach-us/|website=SCOCAblog|publisher=UC Berkeley Constitution Law Center and Hastings Law Journal|accessdate=June 6, 2017|date=November 10, 2016}}</ref> Newly reelected Governor [[George_Deukmejian|George Deukmejian]] was then able to elevate Associate Justice [[Malcolm_M._Lucas|Malcolm M. Lucas]] to Chief Justice and appoint three new associate justices (one to replace Lucas in his old post and two to replace Reynoso and Grodin).<ref name="Braitman">{{cite book|last1=Braitman|first1=Jacqueline R.|last2=Uelmen|first2=Gerald F.|title=Justice Stanley Mosk: A Life at the Center of California Politics and Justice|date=2013|publisher=McFarland & Co.|location=Jefferson, NC|isbn=9781476600710|pages=224–226|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=G7kWXsKpJTEC&pg=PA224|accessdate=24 December 2015}}</ref> -->

===組成結構===
在1879年至1966年間，法院被分為兩個三人司法委員會，即第一委員會與第二委員會。<ref name="Kelly">See ''People v. Kelly'', [http://online.ceb.com/calcases/C4/40C4t106.htm 40 Cal. 4th 106, 113] (2006), which explains the 1879 constitutional convention's decision to create a seven-justice court with two three-justice departments.</ref><!--The chief justice divided cases evenly between the panels and also decided which cases would be heard ''en banc'' by the Court sitting as a whole.
[[File:Supremecourtofcaliforniamaincourthouse.jpg|thumb]] at the Earl Warren Building and Courthouse, which it shares with the [[California_Courts_of_Appeal|Court of Appeal]] for the First District]]
After a constitutional amendment in 1966, the Court currently sits as a whole (all seven together) when hearing all appeals.  When there is an open seat on the court, or if a justice recuses himself or herself on a given case, justices from the [[California_Courts_of_Appeal|California Courts of Appeal]] are assigned by the chief justice to join the court for individual cases on a rotational basis.

The procedure for when all justices recuse themselves from a case has varied over time.<ref>In a 1972 case, [http://law.justia.com/cases/california/supreme-court/3d/25/474.html ''Mosk v. Superior Court''], 25 Cal. 3d 479 (1979), judges were selected by lot to serve on the panel after all of the Supreme Court justices recused themselves: "The Chief Justice assigned six Court of Appeal justices, who were selected by lot pursuant to an order by the Supreme Court, to act on the petition."</ref> For a 1992 case, the chief justice requested the presiding justice of a Court of Appeal district (different from the one where the case originated) to select six other Court of Appeal justices from his district, and they formed an acting Supreme Court for the purpose of deciding that one case.<ref>''Carma Developers (Cal.), Inc. v. Marathon Development California, Inc.'', [http://online.ceb.com/CalCases/C4/2C4t342.htm 2 Cal. 4th 342] (1992).  All members of the Supreme Court recused themselves from the appeal of the First District's decision, so the Third District sat as an "acting" Supreme Court and gave the final opinion in the case.</ref>  However, in a more recent case where all members of the Court recused themselves when Governor Schwarzenegger sought a writ of mandate (''Schwarzenegger v. Court of Appeal (Epstein)''), seven justices of the Courts of Appeal were selected based on the regular rotational basis, not from the same district, with the most senior one serving as the acting chief justice, and that acting supreme court eventually denied the writ petition.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://appellatecases.courtinfo.ca.gov/search/case/dockets.cfm?dist=0&doc_id=1965249&doc_no=S189114 |title=California Courts - Appellate Court Case Information |author= |date= |work= |publisher=Judicial Council of California |accessdate=January 3, 2011}}</ref>
