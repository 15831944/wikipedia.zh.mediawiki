It is a ''hwagyo'', or an ethnic Chinese school in South Korea. The South Korean government began recognizing ''hwagyo'' as educational institutions in 1998.<ref name=LeeChulwoop105>Lee, Chulwoo. "'Us' and 'Them' in Korean Law" (Chapter 6). In: Cheng, Lucie, Arthur Rosett, and Margaret Woo (editors). ''East Asian Law: Universal Norms and Local Cultures''. [[Routledge|Routledge]], September 2, 2003. ISBN 1134431805, 9781134431809. START: p. [https://books.google.com/books?id=zvCBAgAAQBAJ&pg=PA105 105]. CITED: p. [https://books.google.com/books?id=zvCBAgAAQBAJ&pg=PA119 119].</ref>

The area primary school serving Chinese students is [[Seoul_Chinese_Primary_School|Seoul Chinese Primary School]] in [[Jung-gu,_Seoul|Jung-gu]].

==历史==
1942年'''光华中学'''<!--'''Kwanghwa Middle School'''-->建筑，1945年本学校关门因为[[第二次世界大战|第二次世界大战]]，1948年重新开启在中华民国大使馆。<ref name=Kuo/>

{{transh}}
Sun Shu-i became the principal in 1984 and discontinued tracking students into classes A, B, and C.<ref name=Kuo/>

{{Asof|2003}}, the school had 1,100 students. About half of the families of the students made their living as owners or employees of Chinese restaurants.<ref name=LeeChulwoop105/>
